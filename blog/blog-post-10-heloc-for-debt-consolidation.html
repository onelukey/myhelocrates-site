Using a HELOC to Pay Off Debt: Is It a Smart Move?

May 2026 9 min read MyHelocRates Team

Using a HELOC to pay off high-interest debt can save thousands of dollars in interest — but it also converts unsecured debt into a loan backed by your home. The savings are real. So is the risk. Here's how to decide if it makes sense for you.

With credit card rates averaging 20–24% and HELOC rates typically sitting at 7–9%, the interest rate gap is significant. Many homeowners use this difference to their advantage — consolidating high-interest debt into a lower-rate HELOC and dramatically reducing their monthly interest burden. But the strategy has a serious downside that every homeowner needs to understand before proceeding.

How Debt Consolidation with a HELOC Works

The mechanics are straightforward:

  1. You open a HELOC against your home equity
  2. You draw from the HELOC to pay off your credit cards, personal loans, or other high-interest debt in full
  3. You now owe the same total amount — but at a much lower interest rate
  4. You make monthly payments on the HELOC instead of multiple high-rate creditors

The key insight is that you're not eliminating the debt — you're refinancing it at a lower rate using your home equity as collateral. The savings come entirely from paying less interest over time.

Unsecured vs Secured Debt

Credit card debt is unsecured — if you can't pay, the lender can't take your home. A HELOC is secured by your home. This is the central trade-off of the strategy: you get a lower rate, but you're putting your home on the line for debt that wasn't previously tied to it.

When This Strategy Makes Sense

Debt consolidation with a HELOC works best when several conditions are true at the same time:

ConditionWhy It Matters
You have significant high-interest debtThe larger the balance, the bigger the interest saving — smaller balances may not justify the effort and risk
The rate gap is at least 8–10%A small rate difference may not outweigh the risks and costs of setting up the HELOC
You have a clear repayment planThe HELOC must be paid off, not treated as an extended line of cheap credit
You've addressed the spending habits that created the debtIf you run the cards back up after paying them off, you'll end up with both a HELOC balance and new credit card debt
Your income is stableYour home is now collateral — a job loss affecting your ability to repay has more serious consequences

The Biggest Risk: Running Up Debt Again

This is the trap that catches many homeowners — and it deserves its own section.

When you pay off your credit cards with a HELOC, those cards are now at zero balance. They're still open. They're still in your wallet. Unless you've fundamentally changed the spending behaviour that created the debt, there's a real risk you'll run them back up — leaving you with both a HELOC balance and a fresh pile of credit card debt. This is significantly worse than where you started.

The #1 Rule: Close or Cut Up the Cards

Many financial advisors recommend closing the paid-off cards or at minimum cutting them up after consolidating. If you can't trust yourself not to use them, remove the temptation. The savings from the strategy are completely wiped out if you accumulate new high-rate debt on top.

How to Do It Right — Step by Step

  1. List all debts you want to consolidate

    Include balance, interest rate, and minimum payment for each. This becomes your target payoff list.

  2. Calculate the total and confirm your HELOC can cover it

    Make sure your available HELOC credit line is sufficient to pay off the combined balance in full.

  3. Draw from the HELOC and pay off each debt directly

    Don't transfer to a bank account and spend — pay the creditors directly. Highest-rate debt first.

  4. Confirm zero balances in writing

    Get written confirmation from each creditor that the account is paid in full. Keep these records.

  5. Set up autopay on the HELOC immediately

    Pay more than the interest-only minimum every month. Set a target payoff date and stick to it.

  6. Decide what to do with the paid-off cards

    Close them, freeze them, or lock them away. Make a deliberate decision — don't just leave them sitting in your wallet.

Consider a Balance Transfer First for Smaller Balances

If your total debt is under $10,000–$15,000, a 0% balance transfer credit card may be simpler and safer — no home equity at risk, and you avoid HELOC setup costs. The key is to pay it off before the promotional rate expires.

Tax Implications to Be Aware Of

Unlike using a HELOC for home improvements, using HELOC funds for debt consolidation does not qualify for the mortgage interest deduction. Under current tax law, HELOC interest is only deductible when the funds are used to buy, build, or substantially improve the home that secures the loan.

Paying off credit cards or personal loans with a HELOC does not meet this test — so don't factor a tax deduction into your savings calculation for this strategy.

Keep Clean Records if You Mix Uses

If you use the same HELOC for both home improvements and debt consolidation, you must keep separate records of each draw to correctly calculate any deductible interest. Mixed-use HELOCs complicate tax filings — consult a tax professional.

Key Takeaways

  • The interest rate savings can be substantial — especially on large credit card balances
  • The critical risk: your home becomes collateral for debt that wasn't previously secured
  • The strategy only works if you have a clear repayment plan and don't run up new debt
  • Close or remove access to paid-off cards immediately after consolidating
  • HELOC interest for debt consolidation is NOT tax deductible — unlike home improvement use
  • For smaller balances, a 0% balance transfer card is often safer and simpler
