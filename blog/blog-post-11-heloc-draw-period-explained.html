What is a HELOC Draw Period? Everything You Need to Know

May 2026 8 min read MyHelocRates Team

The draw period is the phase of your HELOC when you can actively borrow money. Most last 10 years — but what happens during that time, and what comes after, is something every HELOC borrower needs to understand before signing up.

A HELOC has two distinct phases: the draw period and the repayment period. They work very differently, and the transition between them can catch homeowners off guard if they haven't planned ahead. This guide explains exactly how both phases work, what your payment obligations are in each, and how to avoid the most common pitfall — payment shock.

The Two Phases of a HELOC

Every HELOC is split into two distinct phases that work completely differently from each other:

Draw Period

  • Borrow as much or as little as you need
  • Repay and re-borrow like a credit card
  • Typically interest-only payments required
  • Usually lasts 5–10 years
  • Lower monthly payments
  • Maximum flexibility

Repayment Period

  • No new draws allowed
  • Must repay outstanding balance in full
  • Principal + interest payments required
  • Usually lasts 10–20 years
  • Higher monthly payments
  • Balance must reach zero

How the Draw Period Works in Detail

During the draw period, your HELOC functions like a revolving line of credit — similar to a credit card but secured against your home. Here's what that means in practice:

You Control When and How Much You Borrow

You don't have to draw any funds at all if you don't need them. Many homeowners open a HELOC as a financial safety net and never draw a single dollar. Others draw the full line immediately. Most fall somewhere in between — drawing funds as needs arise and repaying between draws.

Interest-Only Payments Are Standard

During the draw period, most lenders only require you to pay the interest on your outstanding balance each month — not any principal. This keeps monthly payments low, which is one of the HELOC's major attractions.

Interest-Only Doesn't Mean the Debt Is Going Away

If you make only interest payments for 10 years, you'll end the draw period owing exactly as much as you borrowed. The full principal then needs to be repaid during the repayment period — which is where payment shock comes from.

Your Available Credit Replenishes as You Repay

Unlike a term loan, repaying your HELOC balance during the draw period frees up that credit again. If you have a $50,000 line, draw $30,000, and repay $10,000, you now have $30,000 available again. This revolving nature is what makes HELOCs so flexible for ongoing projects.

Understanding Payment Shock — and How to Avoid It

Payment shock is the jarring increase in monthly payments that happens when the draw period ends and principal repayment begins. It catches many homeowners off guard because the interest-only payments during the draw period feel very manageable — and then the bill suddenly doubles or triples.

Payment Shock Calculator

How to Avoid Payment Shock

  • Pay more than interest-only during the draw period — even an extra $100–$200/month toward principal makes a meaningful difference at transition
  • Set a personal payoff target — aim to have the balance at or near zero before the draw period ends
  • Refinance before the draw period ends — if your balance is large, refinancing into a new HELOC or a fixed home equity loan can reset your timeline
  • Plan for the higher payment from day one — budget as if you're already in the repayment period; the difference goes to principal

What Happens If You Can't Pay When the Draw Period Ends?

If you find yourself approaching the end of the draw period with a large balance and no clear repayment plan, you have options — but time matters:

Start Planning 2–3 Years Before Your Draw Period Ends

Don't wait until the final months. Review your HELOC statement annually, note the draw period end date, and start modelling your repayment payment at least 2–3 years out. This gives you time to adjust, refinance, or pay down the balance without being rushed.

Key Takeaways

  • The draw period (typically 10 years) is when you can borrow, repay, and re-borrow freely
  • Most lenders only require interest-only payments during the draw period — but this means your balance doesn't shrink
  • When the draw period ends, payments jump significantly — this is payment shock
  • Plan for the transition at least 2–3 years in advance
  • Paying more than the interest-only minimum during the draw period is the simplest way to protect yourself
  • If you're approaching the end of your draw period with a large balance, refinancing options are available — act early
