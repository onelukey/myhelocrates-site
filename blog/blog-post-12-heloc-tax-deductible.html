Is HELOC Interest Tax Deductible in 2026? The Full Answer

May 2026 8 min read MyHelocRates Team

HELOC interest can be tax deductible — but only when the borrowed funds are used to buy, build, or substantially improve the home that secures the loan. Use the money for anything else, and the deduction doesn't apply.

This is one of the most searched questions about HELOCs — and one of the most misunderstood. The rules changed significantly with the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, and many homeowners are still working from outdated assumptions. This guide gives you the current rules, clear examples, and the records you need to keep to claim the deduction correctly.

This Is Not Tax Advice

Tax rules are complex and your individual situation matters. This article explains the general rules as they stand for most taxpayers in 2026. Always consult a qualified tax professional or CPA before making tax-related decisions about your HELOC.

The Core Rule: It Depends on How You Use the Money

Under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA), which remains in effect for 2026, HELOC interest is deductible only if the loan proceeds are used to "buy, build, or substantially improve" the qualified residence that secures the HELOC.

The key word is substantially. Routine maintenance doesn't count. A major renovation does. The IRS looks at how the money was actually spent — not where it came from or what you called it.

Generally Deductible

  • Kitchen or bathroom renovation
  • Room addition or extension
  • New roof, windows, or siding
  • HVAC system replacement
  • Installing a home office (if qualifying)
  • Structural repairs or improvements
  • Adding a garage or deck

Not Deductible

  • Paying off credit card debt
  • College tuition or student loans
  • Buying a car or boat
  • Medical bills
  • Vacations or personal spending
  • Business expenses (separate rules apply)
  • Investing in stocks or funds

The Itemising Requirement — The Hidden Catch

Even if you use your HELOC entirely for home improvements, you can only benefit from the interest deduction if you itemise deductions on Schedule A of your federal tax return — rather than taking the standard deduction.

The 2017 tax reform nearly doubled the standard deduction. For 2026, the standard deduction is approximately:

Filing StatusStandard Deduction (approx. 2026)
Single~$15,000
Married filing jointly~$30,000
Head of household~$22,500

For the HELOC interest deduction to actually benefit you, your total itemised deductions — including mortgage interest, HELOC interest, state and local taxes (capped at $10,000), and charitable contributions — must exceed your standard deduction. For many middle-income homeowners, this bar is now difficult to clear, which means the deduction has no practical value even if they technically qualify for it.

Most Homeowners Don't Itemise Anymore

Since the 2017 tax reform, the majority of US taxpayers take the standard deduction. If that's you, the HELOC interest deduction has no impact on your tax bill — even if you use the funds for qualifying home improvements. Run the numbers with your tax preparer before factoring a deduction into your financial planning.

The Debt Limit — How Much Interest You Can Deduct

Even if you itemise and use funds for qualifying purposes, the deduction is subject to an overall debt limit. You can only deduct interest on home acquisition debt (your mortgage plus any HELOC used for home improvement) up to a combined total of $750,000 for most taxpayers ($375,000 if married filing separately).

For the vast majority of HELOC borrowers, this limit won't be an issue. But if you have a large first mortgage plus a significant HELOC, your combined debt could approach or exceed the cap.

What About Mixed-Use HELOCs?

Many homeowners use the same HELOC for both qualifying and non-qualifying purposes — for example, $40,000 for a kitchen renovation and $20,000 to pay off car loans. In this case, only the interest attributable to the qualifying portion ($40,000) is deductible.

Mixed Use Requires Meticulous Record-Keeping

If you use your HELOC for both qualifying and non-qualifying purposes, you must track each draw separately and calculate the proportion of interest that's deductible. This is where good records become essential — and where a tax professional earns their fee.

Records You Must Keep to Claim the Deduction

The IRS can ask you to prove that your HELOC funds were used for qualifying home improvements. Keep these records from the moment you open the HELOC:

  • HELOC statements — showing each draw date and amount
  • Contractor invoices and contracts — showing work done and amounts paid
  • Bank records or cancelled cheques — showing payments to contractors
  • Receipts for materials — if you did any work yourself
  • Before and after photos — helpful supporting evidence for major renovations
  • Building permits — for structural work that required local approval
  • Your Form 1098 — from your lender showing total mortgage interest paid
Create a Simple "HELOC Project File"

Keep a dedicated folder — physical or digital — for every draw you make. Include the draw date, amount, purpose, and supporting invoices. This makes tax time straightforward and gives you clear documentation if you're ever audited.

What If the TCJA Expires?

Many provisions of the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act were set to expire after 2025. As of mid-2026, legislative decisions about extensions are ongoing. It's worth noting that before the TCJA, HELOC interest was deductible regardless of how the funds were used (subject to debt limits). If the law changes, the rules around HELOC interest deductibility could broaden. Consult a tax professional for the most current guidance for your situation.

Key Takeaways

  • HELOC interest is deductible only when funds are used to buy, build, or substantially improve your home
  • You must itemise deductions — the standard deduction must be lower than your total itemised deductions
  • Since the 2017 tax reform, most taxpayers take the standard deduction and get no benefit from this rule
  • The combined debt limit for deductible mortgage interest is $750,000
  • Mixed-use HELOCs require careful record-keeping to calculate the deductible portion
  • Always consult a tax professional — individual circumstances vary significantly
