HELOC vs Home Equity Loan — Which Should You Choose?

Both a HELOC and a home equity loan let you borrow against the equity in your home at a lower interest rate than most other forms of borrowing. But they are very different products — and choosing the wrong one for your situation can cost you significantly more money, or leave you without the flexibility you need. This guide cuts through the confusion with a clear, honest comparison.

The key difference in one sentence

A HELOC is a revolving credit line you draw from as needed — like a credit card backed by your home. A home equity loan gives you a fixed lump sum at a locked interest rate — like a traditional loan secured against your home.

Quick decision rule

Ongoing or uncertain costs? → HELOC. Single known expense? → Home equity loan. Keep reading to understand why — and when the exceptions apply.

How each product works

How a HELOC works

A HELOC gives you a maximum credit limit based on your home equity. During the draw period (typically 5–10 years) you can borrow up to that limit, repay it, and borrow again as many times as you like. You only pay interest on the amount you have actually drawn — not on the full limit. After the draw period, you enter a repayment period (typically 10–20 years) where you repay the outstanding balance with principal and interest payments.

How a home equity loan works

A home equity loan gives you the full approved amount at closing as a lump sum, at a fixed interest rate. You begin repaying immediately with equal monthly payments covering both principal and interest. The rate and payment never change for the life of the loan. There is no draw period — you receive the money once and repay it on a fixed schedule.

The partial use advantage

If you only end up needing $40,000 of an $80,000 facility, the HELOC is dramatically cheaper — you only pay interest on what you drew. With a home equity loan you are paying interest on the full $80,000 from day one regardless of how much you use.

When to choose a HELOC

✓ Choose HELOC

Multi-stage renovation

Kitchen this year, bathroom next — draw only what each stage costs rather than the full budget upfront.

✓ Choose HELOC

Emergency fund backstop

Open the line and leave it unused. Zero cost until you actually need it — the ideal safety net.

✓ Choose HELOC

Uncertain total cost

When you cannot predict the final bill — ongoing medical costs, business expenses, evolving projects.

✓ Choose HELOC

Rates expected to fall

A variable HELOC benefits automatically from Fed rate cuts — no refinancing needed.

When to choose a home equity loan

✓ Choose Home Equity Loan

Single known large expense

A specific project with a defined cost — full kitchen remodel, roof replacement, solar installation.

✓ Choose Home Equity Loan

Debt consolidation payoff

Paying off a specific list of debts at closing — a lump sum makes budgeting clean and simple.

✓ Choose Home Equity Loan

You need payment certainty

Fixed income, tight budget, or you simply cannot risk payment increases if rates rise.

✓ Choose Home Equity Loan

Rates expected to rise

Lock in today's rate before further increases. A fixed loan is fully protected from Fed hikes.

Frequently asked questions

Can I have both a HELOC and a home equity loan at the same time?
Technically yes, but most lenders will only allow one additional home equity product at a time. Having both would increase your combined loan-to-value ratio significantly, potentially pushing it above the lender's maximum. In practice, most homeowners choose one product. If you need both flexibility and a fixed amount, consider a HELOC with a fixed-rate lock option instead.
Which is easier to qualify for?
Qualification requirements are very similar for both products — credit score, home equity, debt-to-income ratio, and income verification requirements are essentially the same. The main differences are in how the product works after approval rather than in the qualification process. See our full qualification guide for details.
Is a home equity loan safer than a HELOC?
Both products use your home as collateral, so both carry the risk of foreclosure if you default. A home equity loan could be considered lower risk in a rising rate environment because your payment is fixed and cannot increase. A HELOC carries more payment uncertainty due to its variable rate. However, a HELOC also carries less risk of overborrowing since you draw only what you need — whereas a home equity loan delivers the full amount immediately.
Can I convert a home equity loan to a HELOC?
Not directly — they are separate products and you cannot convert one to the other. However, you could pay off your home equity loan using a new HELOC if you wanted more flexibility going forward, subject to qualifying and available equity. There would be closing costs involved in opening the new HELOC. Consult a licensed mortgage professional to evaluate whether this makes sense for your situation.
