Using a HELOC for Home Improvements: The Smart Homeowner's Guide

May 2026 9 min read Mike Lucas — Founder

Home improvement is the most popular use for a HELOC — and for good reason. You borrow only what you need, when you need it, at interest rates far lower than credit cards or personal loans. Done right, the renovations can even increase your home's value.

Using your home equity to improve your home is one of the few borrowing strategies where the money you spend can genuinely come back to you. But there's a right way and a wrong way to go about it. This guide covers everything: which projects give you the best return, how to structure your draws, tax considerations, and the mistakes that catch homeowners off guard.

Why a HELOC Makes Sense for Home Renovations

Home improvement projects often don't have a precise price tag upfront. Contractors uncover unexpected issues, material costs change, and the scope of work tends to expand. A HELOC is uniquely suited to this reality because of how it works:

  • Revolving credit line — draw what you need, when you need it, not one lump sum up front
  • Interest only on what you draw — if your $50,000 line sits mostly unused, you only pay interest on the amount outstanding
  • Lower rates than alternatives — HELOC rates are typically far below credit cards, personal loans, or contractor financing
  • Potential tax deduction — interest may be deductible if the funds are used to "buy, build, or substantially improve" your home (see tax section below)
HELOC vs Construction Loan

For major structural projects or new builds, a dedicated construction loan may be more appropriate. HELOCs work best for renovations on an existing, already-mortgaged property.

The 80% Rule of Thumb

Industry professionals often say the best renovations should recoup at least 70–80% of their cost at resale. Anything below 50% is a lifestyle purchase, not an investment. A HELOC is great for both — just know which category you're in before you borrow.

How to Draw HELOC Funds Smartly

One of the biggest advantages of a HELOC over a lump-sum loan is that you control the timing of your draws. Here's how to use that flexibility to your advantage:

  1. Draw only when a contractor bill is due

    Don't pull the full credit line on day one. Draw just before each stage payment — foundation, framing, finishing — so you minimise the balance accruing interest.

  2. Keep a buffer for overruns

    Budget a contingency of 10–20% above your project estimate. Keep this amount undrawn but accessible — renovation overruns are extremely common.

  3. Pay back as you go

    During the draw period, make payments above the interest-only minimum whenever you can. This frees up your line for the next draw and reduces total interest paid.

  4. Track your draws by project phase

    Keep a simple spreadsheet matching each draw to a specific project phase and contractor invoice. You'll need this for tax purposes if claiming interest deductions.

  5. Don't mix uses in one draw

    If you also plan to use your HELOC for non-home-improvement purposes, keep separate records. Mixed-use draws can complicate interest deductibility calculations.

The Tax Angle: Is HELOC Interest Deductible?

This is one of the most misunderstood aspects of HELOCs. Here's the plain-English version:

Under current tax law (as of 2026), HELOC interest is deductible on your federal tax return only if the borrowed funds are used to "buy, build, or substantially improve" the home that secures the loan. Using a HELOC for home renovations on your primary or secondary home generally qualifies.

Deductibility Is Not Automatic

You must itemise deductions on Schedule A (rather than taking the standard deduction) for HELOC interest to benefit you. For most households, the standard deduction is larger, making the mortgage interest deduction irrelevant. Consult a tax professional to understand your specific situation.

Common Mistakes Homeowners Make

Mistake 1: Drawing the full line immediately

HELOC interest accrues from the day you draw funds. Drawing $80,000 on day one when your contractor won't bill until month three means paying unnecessary interest. Draw in stages aligned to your project timeline.

Mistake 2: Ignoring the repayment period

Most HELOCs have a 10-year draw period followed by a 20-year repayment period where you can no longer draw funds and must repay principal. If your HELOC balance is large when the draw period ends, your monthly payment can jump significantly. Plan ahead.

Mistake 3: Over-improving for your neighbourhood

A $150,000 kitchen renovation in a neighbourhood of $400,000 homes won't generate a $150,000 increase in value. Buyers compare homes to nearby listings. Know your market ceiling before borrowing.

Mistake 4: Not getting contractor bids in writing

Verbal estimates routinely expand. Always get a fixed-price or not-to-exceed contract in writing before drawing funds. This also protects you legally if work is incomplete or substandard.

Key Takeaways

  • HELOCs are ideal for home renovations because you draw funds as needed, not all at once
  • Kitchen, bathroom, and curb appeal projects tend to offer the best returns at resale
  • HELOC interest may be tax-deductible for qualifying home improvement use — keep records
  • Draw in stages, keep a contingency buffer, and never over-improve for your market
  • Have a repayment plan in place before the draw period ends to avoid payment shock
