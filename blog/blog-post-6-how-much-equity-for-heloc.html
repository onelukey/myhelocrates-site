How Much Home Equity Do You Need for a HELOC?

May 2026 8 min read MyHelocRates Team

Most lenders require you to retain at least 20% equity in your home after taking out a HELOC — meaning you can typically borrow up to 80% of your home's value, minus any existing mortgage balance.

Before you can tap into your home's value with a HELOC, you need to meet the equity threshold lenders set. But what exactly does that mean, and how do you calculate how much you can actually access? This guide breaks it all down in plain English.

20% Minimum equity most lenders require you to keep
80% Typical maximum combined LTV (CLTV) allowed
85%+ Some lenders go up to 85–90% CLTV

What Is Home Equity?

Home equity is simply the difference between what your home is currently worth and what you still owe on your mortgage.

For example: if your home is worth $400,000 and you owe $250,000 on your mortgage, you have $150,000 in equity — that's 37.5% of the home's value.

Equity builds in two ways:

  • Paying down your mortgage — each monthly payment reduces your balance
  • Home value appreciation — if prices in your area rise, so does your equity

How Much Equity Do You Need to Qualify?

Lenders don't let you borrow against 100% of your equity. They require you to maintain a cushion — typically at least 20% of the home's value — to protect themselves if property values fall.

This is measured using your Combined Loan-to-Value ratio (CLTV) — the total of all loans secured by your home divided by your home's current market value.

Lender Type Typical Max CLTV Equity You Must Keep
Most banks & credit unions 80% 20%
Some online lenders 85% 15%
Select lenders (strong credit) 90% 10%
Navy Federal / USAA (members) 95% 5%
Higher CLTV = Higher Risk

Lenders offering 85–90% CLTV will typically require excellent credit scores (720+) and charge higher interest rates to compensate for the increased risk.

How to Calculate Your Available HELOC Credit

Use this simple three-step formula:

  1. Home value × maximum CLTV = your lending ceiling
  2. Lending ceiling − existing mortgage balance = your maximum HELOC line
  3. Confirm this is above your lender's minimum draw amount (often $10,000–$25,000)

What If You Don't Have Enough Equity Yet?

If your CLTV is too high to qualify right now, you're not out of options. Here are the most practical paths forward:

1. Pay Down Your Mortgage Faster

Making extra principal payments — even an additional $100–$200 per month — can meaningfully reduce your loan balance over time and push your CLTV below lender thresholds. Check that your mortgage has no prepayment penalties first.

2. Wait for Home Value Appreciation

In rising real estate markets, your home may reach the required equity threshold without you doing anything. Get a new appraisal or use an online home value estimator periodically to check your progress.

3. Consider a Cash-Out Refinance

If interest rates are favourable and you want a lump sum, a cash-out refinance replaces your entire mortgage with a new, larger one. It's not a HELOC, but it accomplishes a similar goal if your equity is borderline.

4. Look at Lenders with Higher CLTV Limits

Some credit unions and online lenders go up to 85–90% CLTV. If you have strong credit and stable income, shopping around may find you a lender willing to work with your current equity level.

Pro Tip: Get a Fresh Appraisal

If your home has appreciated since your last appraisal, you may qualify for a larger HELOC line than you expect. Many lenders will accept a professional appraisal, a broker price opinion, or their own automated valuation model (AVM).

How Lenders Actually Verify Your Home's Value

You can't simply tell a lender what your home is worth — they'll verify it independently. Common methods include:

  • Full appraisal — a licensed appraiser visits your home; most accurate but costs $300–$600
  • Desktop appraisal — appraiser uses data only, no site visit; faster and cheaper
  • Automated Valuation Model (AVM) — lender uses an algorithm; common for straightforward applications
  • Drive-by appraisal — appraiser views exterior only; used for lower CLTV applications
Online Estimates Aren't Enough

Zillow, Redfin, and similar sites provide useful ballpark figures, but lenders won't use them. Get a professional appraisal if you're close to the equity threshold — it could be the difference between approval and rejection.

Other Equity-Related Factors Lenders Consider

Equity isn't the only box you need to tick. Lenders will also look at:

Factor Typical Requirement
Credit score 620 minimum; 700+ for best rates
Debt-to-income ratio (DTI) 43% maximum; under 36% preferred
Income verification 2 years W-2s or tax returns
Property type Primary residences most favoured
Payment history No recent late mortgage payments

Key Takeaways

  • Most lenders require you to keep at least 20% equity, limiting your CLTV to 80%
  • Use the formula: (Home Value × Max CLTV) − Mortgage Balance = your HELOC limit
  • Some lenders go up to 85–90% CLTV if you have excellent credit
  • If you fall short, paying extra principal or waiting for appreciation are the main paths forward
  • Always get a professional appraisal if you're close to the threshold
